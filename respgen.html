Rationale:

Potential Condition: The most likely condition is Sepsis-induced organ dysfunction (specifically manifesting as acute respiratory failure or early ARDS). This is a more encompassing diagnosis than simply worsening pneumonia.

Question 2: Case Study

A 72-year-old female with a history of severe COPD and a 50-pack-year smoking history is admitted to the medical floor with increasing shortness of breath, a productive cough with greenish sputum, and wheezing. She reports feeling more tired and confused than usual over the past day.

Temperature: 100.9°F (38.3°C)
Heart Rate: 118 beats/min
Respiratory Rate: 28 breaths/min, shallow
Blood Pressure: 148/88 mmHg
Oxygen Saturation: 86% on 2 L/min nasal cannula

Arterial Blood Gas (on 2L NC):
pH: 7.28
PaCO2: 68 mmHg
PaO2: 55 mmHg
HCO3: 29 mEq/L

The nurse has analyzed the client data. Which three actions should the nurse take immediately?

Increase oxygen to 6 L/min via nasal cannula.
Initiate BiPAP therapy as per protocol.
Administer prescribed albuterol nebulizer treatment.
Place the client in the supine position.
Notify the Rapid Response Team (RRT).
Administer a PRN sedative for anxiety.

Rationale: The client is in acute respiratory failure secondary to a COPD exacerbation. The ABGs show a partially compensated respiratory acidosis with significant hypercapnia and hypoxemia.
1. Initiate BiPAP therapy as per protocol: Correct. BiPAP will help decrease the work of breathing, improve alveolar ventilation to blow off CO2, and improve oxygenation. It is a key intervention for hypercapnic respiratory failure in COPD.
2. Administer prescribed albuterol nebulizer treatment: Correct. This will address the bronchospasm contributing to the exacerbation, helping to open the airways.
3. Notify the Rapid Response Team (RRT): Correct. The client is showing signs of impending respiratory collapse (confusion, severe acidosis, hypoxemia despite O2). The RRT is needed for a higher level of care and potential intubation.
Incorrect options: Increasing O2 significantly via nasal cannula can worsen CO2 retention in some COPD patients. The supine position will worsen breathing. A sedative is contraindicated as it will further depress the respiratory drive.

Question 3: Extended Drag and Drop

A nurse is preparing to care for a client with pneumonia. The provider has written several orders. The nurse must decide which interventions are necessary and categorize them based on priority.

Provider Orders

Obtain sputum culture
Administer IV ceftriaxone
Encourage use of incentive spirometer
Administer acetaminophen for fever > 101°F
Administer enoxaparin for VTE prophylaxis
Consult physical therapy for mobility

Priority Interventions

Highest Priority (Must do first)
Secondary Priority
Lower Priority

Rationale:

The correct prioritization is based on the principles of infection control and the ABCs (Airway, Breathing, Circulation).

  1. Highest Priority: Obtain sputum culture. This must be done *before* administering the first dose of antibiotics to ensure the culture is not skewed by the medication, allowing for targeted therapy later if needed.
  2. Secondary Priority: Administer IV ceftriaxone. Starting antibiotics promptly is critical to treating the underlying infection and preventing sepsis.
  3. Lower Priority: Encourage use of incentive spirometer, administer acetaminophen, and administer enoxaparin. These are all important interventions for managing symptoms, preventing complications (like atelectasis and VTE), and promoting recovery, but they follow the critical steps of diagnostics and antimicrobial administration. The physical therapy consult can be done last.

Question 4: Matrix/Grid

A nurse is caring for four clients on a respiratory unit. For each client, determine if the finding requires immediate intervention by the nurse.

Client Finding Requires Immediate Intervention Does Not Require Immediate Intervention
A client with asthma reports using their albuterol inhaler 3 times in the past hour with little relief.
A client with COPD has an SpO2 of 91% and is using their accessory muscles to breathe.
A client receiving mechanical ventilation for ARDS has a PEEP of 15 cm H2O and a blood pressure of 85/50 mmHg.
A postoperative client has fine crackles in the lung bases and a respiratory rate of 20 breaths/min.

Rationale:

  • Asthma client: Requires Immediate Intervention. Using a rescue inhaler frequently with little relief indicates status asthmaticus, a medical emergency.
  • COPD client: Requires Immediate Intervention. While 91% SpO2 may be at baseline for this client, the use of accessory muscles signifies a significant increase in the work of breathing and respiratory distress.
  • ARDS client: Requires Immediate Intervention. High levels of PEEP can decrease venous return and cause hypotension. This blood pressure is dangerously low and requires immediate action (e.g., notifying the provider, preparing to administer fluids or vasopressors).
  • Postoperative client: Does Not Require Immediate Intervention. Fine crackles in the bases are a common finding post-op, representing atelectasis. While it requires intervention (e.g., coughing, deep breathing, incentive spirometry), it is not a medical emergency requiring the nurse's *immediate* action over the others.

Question 5: Cloze (Drop-down)

A nurse is caring for a client who has just been intubated for acute respiratory failure. The nurse confirms tube placement by checking for end-tidal CO2, auscultating for bilateral breath sounds, and getting a chest x-ray. The initial ventilator settings are being applied. The nurse understands that the is the amount of air delivered with each breath and is set based on the client's ideal body weight to prevent barotrauma. The is the concentration of oxygen delivered, which is initially set high and then titrated down based on ABGs. Finally, a small amount of is often used to keep the alveoli open at the end of expiration, which improves oxygenation.

Rationale:

1. Tidal Volume (VT): This is the correct term for the volume of air delivered with each breath.

2. FiO2: This stands for the fraction of inspired oxygen and represents the oxygen concentration being delivered.

3. PEEP (Positive End-Expiratory Pressure): This is the pressure maintained in the airways at the end of exhalation to prevent alveolar collapse.
