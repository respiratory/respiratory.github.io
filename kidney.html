Key Concepts

Kidney/Urinary Assessment

Focus on urine output, edema, weight changes, pain, vital signs, and lab values.

AKI Lab Values

Monitor serum creatinine, BUN, potassium, sodium, calcium, phosphate, GFR, and blood gases.

AKI Risk Factors

Includes hypovolemia, sepsis, nephrotoxic drugs, heart failure, and hypotension.

AKI Prevention

Hydration, avoiding nephrotoxins, monitoring BP and urine output.

AKI Priorities of Care

Fluid management, electrolyte balance, hemodynamic stability, and dialysis preparation if needed.

Fluid Balance Indicator

Daily weight is the most accurate indicator.

Urinary Tract Disorders

UTI Contributing Factors

Poor hygiene, urinary retention, catheter use, diabetes, dehydration.

UTI Prevention

Proper perineal care, adequate fluid intake, early catheter removal, avoiding bladder irritants.

Nursing Process for Lower UTI

Assessment, antibiotics, symptom relief, education.

Urinary Obstruction

Causes include kidney stones, strictures, tumors, BPH; treatment varies from medications to surgery.

Nursing Process for Renal Calculi

Pain control, hydration, urine straining, dietary changes.

Urinary Diversion

Monitor stoma, prevent infection, educate on appliance care.

Quiz

Answer the following questions in 2-3 sentences each.

1. A patient with AKI has a serum potassium level of 6.2 mEq/L. Explain why this is a concern and what immediate assessment should the nurse prioritize.

A serum potassium level of 6.2 mEq/L is dangerously high (hyperkalemia) and can lead to cardiac dysrhythmias. The nurse should immediately assess the patient's cardiac status, including heart rate and rhythm, and prepare for interventions to lower potassium levels.

2. Describe the difference between prerenal and intrarenal acute kidney injury, including a potential cause for each.

Prerenal AKI is caused by reduced blood flow to the kidneys, such as from hypovolemia or hypotension. Intrarenal AKI involves direct damage to the kidney tissue itself, which can be caused by nephrotoxic medications or glomerulonephritis.

3. What are the primary goals of hemodialysis in a patient with acute kidney injury or end-stage kidney disease? Briefly explain the principles of diffusion and ultrafiltration in this context.

The primary goals of hemodialysis are to remove waste products (such as urea and creatinine) and excess fluid from the blood. Diffusion is the movement of solutes from an area of high concentration to low concentration, while ultrafiltration is the removal of fluid due to a pressure gradient.

4. A postoperative kidney transplant patient has a sudden decrease in urine output. What are the immediate nursing actions and potential complications the nurse should suspect?

A sudden decrease in urine output in a postoperative kidney transplant patient is concerning for potential complications such as dehydration, rejection, a kinked or obstructed urinary catheter, or vascular issues with the new kidney. The nurse should immediately assess the catheter patency, vital signs, and notify the physician.

5. List three common signs and symptoms a patient with a lower urinary tract infection might report during an assessment.

Common signs and symptoms of a lower urinary tract infection include dysuria (painful urination), urinary frequency, urgency, and potentially hematuria (blood in the urine).

6. Explain why increased fluid intake is often recommended for patients with urinary tract infections and renal calculi, unless contraindicated.

Increased fluid intake helps to dilute the urine, making it less irritating to the bladder and urethra in UTIs, and aids in flushing out bacteria and small stones in cases of renal calculi. This reduces the risk of infection and stone progression.

7. What are some atypical symptoms of a urinary tract infection that might be observed in an elderly patient? Why is it important to recognize these?

Atypical symptoms of a UTI in elderly patients can include confusion, weakness or fatigue, and anorexia. Recognizing these is important because elderly individuals may not present with the classic urinary symptoms, delaying diagnosis and treatment.

8. A urinalysis report shows the presence of leukocyte esterase and nitrites. What do these findings suggest, and what is the next likely diagnostic step?

The presence of leukocyte esterase (indicating white blood cells) and nitrites (suggesting the presence of certain bacteria like E. coli) in a urinalysis strongly suggests a urinary tract infection. The next likely diagnostic step is a urine culture and sensitivity to identify the specific organism and determine the appropriate antibiotic.

9. Describe the typical pain associated with renal calculi and its common location. What is an important nursing intervention related to pain management and stone analysis?

The pain associated with renal calculi is typically described as severe, colicky flank pain that may radiate to the groin or abdomen. An important nursing intervention includes administering analgesics for pain relief and straining all urine to collect any passed stones for analysis to help determine the cause.

10. What key patient education points should be included for a patient being discharged home after treatment for a urinary tract infection to prevent recurrence?

Patient education for preventing recurrent UTIs includes encouraging adequate fluid intake, proper hygiene practices (wiping front to back), avoiding bladder irritants, completing the full course of antibiotics, and understanding the signs and symptoms of a UTI that warrant seeking medical attention.

Glossary of Key Terms

AKI (Acute Kidney Injury): Sudden kidney function decline.
Anuria/Oliguria: No or reduced urine output.
AV Fistula/Graft: Dialysis access points.
Azotemia: Excess nitrogenous waste.
CKD (Chronic Kidney Disease): Progressive kidney damage.
Creatinine: Kidney function marker.
Dysuria: Painful urination.
GFR (Glomerular Filtration Rate): Kidney function test.
Hematuria: Blood in urine.
Hemodialysis: Blood filtration treatment.
Hydronephrosis: Kidney swelling due to urine blockage.
Hyperkalemia/Hypokalemia: High/low potassium levels.
Metabolic Acidosis: Acid buildup in kidney failure.
Nephrolithiasis/Urolithiasis: Kidney/urinary tract stones.
Nocturia: Frequent nighttime urination.
Peritoneal Dialysis: Dialysis using abdominal lining.
Prerenal/Postrenal AKI: Due to low perfusion/obstruction.
Pyelonephritis: Kidney infection.
Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT): Dialysis or transplant.
Urinary Diversion: Rerouting urine due to bladder issues.
UTI (Urinary Tract Infection): Infection in the urinary system.
