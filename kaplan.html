Kaplan's RN Decision Tree Guide

Use this interactive guide to understand the 5 steps of Kaplan's RN Decision Tree, a critical tool for breaking down NCLEX questions.

STEP 1: Identify the topic of the question?

Understanding the core subject of the question is the first step. Is it about cardiac care, neurological issues, communication, delegation, etc.? This helps you narrow down your focus.

STEP 2: Are the answers assessment or implementation?

Determine if the options provided are primarily about gathering information (assessment) or taking action (implementation). For an unstable client, implementation is often prioritized over further assessment, but for a stable client with a new issue, assessment might come first.

STEP 3: Apply Maslow: Are the answers physical or psychosocial?

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs guides priorities. Physiological needs (e.g., breathing, circulation, nutrition, pain management) take precedence over safety, love/belonging, esteem, or self-actualization needs.

STEP 4: Are the answer choices related to ABCs?

Airway, Breathing, and Circulation (ABCs) are always top priorities in nursing care. If any answer choice directly addresses a compromise in a client's airway, breathing, or circulation, it is likely the most critical intervention.

STEP 5: What is the outcome of each of the remaining answers?

After applying the previous steps, evaluate the remaining plausible answer choices. Consider the immediate and long-term outcomes of each action. Choose the answer that provides the most beneficial and immediate outcome for the client, asking yourself: **“If I could do only one thing and then leave, what would I do?"**

Additional NCLEX Tips

Who Do You See First? Prioritization Principles

  • **Unstable vs. Stable:** Unstable clients (e.g., declining vital signs, acute distress) take priority over stable ones.
  • **Unexpected vs. Expected:** Unexpected findings (e.g., sudden change in condition) are prioritized over expected post-op symptoms.
  • **ABCs:** Airway, Breathing, Circulation are always the highest priority.
  • **Acute vs. Chronic:** Acute problems generally take precedence over chronic, stable conditions.
  • **Actual vs. Potential:** An actual problem (e.g., active bleeding) takes priority over a potential problem (e.g., risk for fall).

Common NCLEX Traps to Avoid

  • Do not ask "Why?” (Can make clients defensive).
  • Do not leave the client. (Ensure client safety and continuity of care).
  • Do not persuade the client. (Respect client autonomy).
  • Do not say, "Don't worry!” (False reassurance dismisses feelings).
  • Do not pass the buck. (Take responsibility for your actions/assessments).
  • Do not read into the question. (Answer based only on the information given).
  • Do not 'do nothing.' (Always take appropriate nursing action).

Therapeutic Communication Tips

DO:

  • Respond to feeling tone. (Acknowledge emotions).
  • Provide information. (Educate clients as needed).
  • Focus on the client. (Client-centered approach).
  • Use silence. (Allows client time to process and respond).
  • Use presence. (Be fully engaged and attentive).

DO NOT:

  • Ask 'why' questions.
  • Ask 'yes/no' questions, except in the case of possible self-harm. (Limits communication).
  • Focus on the nurse. (Avoid self-disclosure that shifts focus).
  • Explore. (Avoid digging unnecessarily; maintain professional boundaries).
  • Do not say, “Don't worry!"

Rules for Delegation

  • **RN Assignment:** Cannot delegate assessment, teaching, or nursing judgment.
  • **LPN/LVN Assignment:** Assign stable clients with expected outcomes.
  • **UAP Assignment:** Delegate standard, unchanging procedures.

Five Rights of Delegation:

  1. **Right Task:** (Within scope of practice, stable client).
  2. **Right Circumstances:** (Appropriate workload and client condition).
  3. **Right Person:** (Competent and within their scope of practice).
  4. **Right Communication:** (Clear, concise, specific task, expected results, follow-up).
  5. **Right Supervision:** (Monitor, evaluate, intervene if necessary).
