Question 1

Discuss the modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors for cerebrovascular disorders, emphasizing the importance of preventative measures in reducing the incidence and impact of stroke.

Potential Answer Points
Non-Modifiable Risk Factors:
  • Age (Risk increases significantly with age)
  • Gender (Men > Women at younger ages, Women > Men at older ages; Women have higher mortality)
  • Race/Ethnicity (Higher incidence/mortality in African Americans, Hispanic Americans)
  • Family History/Genetics (e.g., history of stroke, genetic disorders affecting blood vessels)
Modifiable Risk Factors:
  • Hypertension (Most important and treatable)
  • Smoking (Damages blood vessels, increases clot formation)
  • Diabetes Mellitus (Accelerates atherosclerosis)
  • Dyslipidemia (High LDL, low HDL, high triglycerides)
  • Atrial Fibrillation (Increases risk of cardiogenic embolic stroke)
  • Other Cardiac Conditions (e.g., heart failure, valve disease)
  • Carotid Artery Stenosis / Atherosclerosis
  • Obesity / Unhealthy Diet (High sodium, saturated fat)
  • Physical Inactivity
  • Excessive Alcohol Consumption
  • Illicit Drug Use (e.g., cocaine, amphetamines)
  • Sleep Apnea
Importance of Prevention:
  • Majority of strokes are linked to modifiable risk factors.
  • Focus on lifestyle changes: Healthy diet (DASH), regular exercise, smoking cessation, moderate alcohol use.
  • Effective medical management of chronic conditions: Controlling BP, blood glucose, lipids; anticoagulation for AFib.
  • Patient education and awareness are crucial for adherence and early recognition of warning signs (TIA).
  • Reduces not only stroke incidence but also long-term disability and healthcare costs.

Question 2

Compare and contrast the clinical manifestations and acute medical management strategies for ischemic and hemorrhagic strokes, highlighting the critical differences in their initial treatment approaches.

Potential Answer Points
Clinical Manifestations (Often Overlap):
  • Sudden onset of neurological deficits.
  • Common: Unilateral weakness/numbness (face, arm, leg), confusion, trouble speaking (dysarthria) or understanding (aphasia), visual disturbances (hemianopsia, diplopia), dizziness, loss of balance/coordination (ataxia).
  • Hemorrhagic strokes *may* present more often with severe headache ("worst headache of my life" - esp. SAH), nausea/vomiting, decreased level of consciousness, and seizures early on, but these can occur in ischemic too.
  • Specific deficits depend on the brain region affected and stroke size/type.
Acute Medical Management Comparison:
  • Initial Steps (Both): ABCs (Airway, Breathing, Circulation), rapid transport to stroke center, immediate non-contrast CT scan, establish time of symptom onset, baseline labs (glucose, coagulation), neurological assessment (NIHSS).
  • Ischemic Stroke Management:
    • Goal: Restore blood flow (reperfusion).
    • Non-Contrast CT: Rules OUT hemorrhage.
    • IV Thrombolysis (t-PA): If within time window (e.g., 3-4.5 hours) and no contraindications. CRITICAL DIFFERENCE.
    • Mechanical Thrombectomy: For large vessel occlusion, often used in conjunction with or after IV t-PA, wider time window possible.
    • Blood Pressure Management: Permissive hypertension often allowed initially (e.g., <220/120) unless t-PA given (then target <185/110 before and <180/105 after).
    • Antiplatelet Therapy: Aspirin usually started within 24-48 hours (after 24h post-t-PA).
    • Supportive care: Glucose control, DVT prophylaxis, swallow evaluation, statin therapy.
  • Hemorrhagic Stroke Management:
    • Goal: Stop bleeding, control BP, manage intracranial pressure (ICP).
    • Non-Contrast CT: Confirms hemorrhage, shows location/size (Intracerebral - ICH vs. Subarachnoid - SAH). CT Angiography may follow to identify source (e.g., aneurysm, AVM).
    • IV Thrombolysis (t-PA): ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED. CRITICAL DIFFERENCE.
    • Blood Pressure Management: Aggressive BP reduction typically needed (e.g., SBP target <140 or <160 depending on specific situation).
    • Reversal of Anticoagulation: If patient was on anticoagulants (e.g., warfarin, DOACs).
    • ICP Management: Head elevation, osmotic therapy (mannitol, hypertonic saline), potential external ventricular drain (EVD).
    • Surgical Intervention: Possible hematoma evacuation (ICH), aneurysm clipping/coiling (SAH).
    • Seizure prophylaxis may be considered.
Key Differences Summary: Use of t-PA/thrombectomy (Ischemic only), BP management goals (Permissive HTN vs. Aggressive reduction), anticoagulation reversal (Hemorrhagic), surgical options (different targets/goals).

Question 3

Describe the nursing process as it applies to the care of a patient in the acute phase of an ischemic stroke, including assessment findings, potential nursing diagnoses, planned interventions, and expected outcomes.

Potential Answer Points
Assessment (Acute Phase):
  • Neurological: NIHSS baseline and frequent reassessments, Level of Consciousness (GCS), pupillary response, motor function (strength, drift), sensory perception, speech/language (aphasia, dysarthria), vision (hemianopsia, neglect), swallowing (dysphagia screen), cranial nerves. Monitor for changes indicating improvement, deterioration, or complications (e.g., hemorrhagic conversion, cerebral edema).
  • Vital Signs: Frequent monitoring of BP (permissive HTN vs. post-t-PA goals), HR, RR, O2 saturation, temperature (hyperthermia worsens outcomes).
  • Respiratory: Airway patency, breathing pattern, lung sounds (risk of aspiration).
  • Cardiovascular: Heart rhythm (monitor for AFib), peripheral circulation.
  • Other: Blood glucose levels (hyper/hypoglycemia), intake/output, skin integrity (risk from immobility), bowel/bladder function.
Potential Nursing Diagnoses:
  • Ineffective Cerebral Tissue Perfusion r/t interruption of blood flow.
  • Risk for Aspiration r/t impaired swallowing (dysphagia).
  • Impaired Physical Mobility r/t hemiparesis/hemiplegia.
  • Impaired Verbal Communication r/t aphasia or dysarthria.
  • Unilateral Neglect r/t sensory-perceptual alterations.
  • Risk for Impaired Skin Integrity r/t immobility and sensory loss.
  • Self-Care Deficit (feeding, bathing, dressing, toileting) r/t neurological impairment.
  • Risk for Injury r/t impaired mobility, sensory deficits, confusion.
  • Anxiety/Fear r/t sudden illness and uncertain prognosis.
Planned Interventions:
  • Administer t-PA if ordered, monitor for bleeding.
  • Maintain HOB elevation per protocol (e.g., 30 degrees unless contraindicated).
  • Implement aspiration precautions (NPO until swallow eval, proper positioning for feeding).
  • Perform frequent neuro checks and vital sign monitoring.
  • Assist with repositioning (q2h), ROM exercises (passive/active-assistive).
  • Use communication aids (picture boards, simple commands) for aphasia.
  • Approach patient from unaffected side, place items in visual field for hemianopsia/neglect.
  • Implement safety measures (bed low, side rails up per policy, call bell accessible).
  • Provide skin care, use pressure-relieving surfaces.
  • Assist with ADLs, encourage independence where possible.
  • Provide emotional support, clear explanations to patient/family.
  • Administer scheduled meds (antiplatelets, statins, antihypertensives, etc.).
  • Monitor blood glucose, administer insulin as needed.
  • Implement DVT prophylaxis (SCDs, anticoagulants if ordered).
Expected Outcomes:
  • Neurological status stabilizes or improves.
  • Adequate airway, oxygenation, and cerebral perfusion maintained.
  • Aspiration is prevented.
  • Complications (hemorrhage, edema, DVT, PE, skin breakdown, contractures) are prevented or managed promptly.
  • Patient maintains maximum possible mobility and self-care function.
  • Communication methods are established.
  • Patient/family verbalize understanding of condition and treatment plan.
  • Safety is maintained.

Question 4

Explain the pathophysiology, clinical manifestations, and critical nursing interventions required for the management of a patient experiencing autonomic dysreflexia.

Potential Answer Points
Pathophysiology:
  • Occurs in patients with Spinal Cord Injury (SCI) at level T6 or above, after spinal shock resolves.
  • Triggered by a noxious (irritating) stimulus *below* the level of injury (e.g., full bladder, impacted bowel, pressure injury, tight clothing).
  • Afferent sensory impulses from the stimulus travel up the spinal cord but are blocked at the injury level.
  • This initiates a massive, unchecked sympathetic reflex response *below* the level of injury, causing widespread vasoconstriction.
  • Vasoconstriction leads to severe, acute hypertension.
  • Baroreceptors in the aortic arch and carotid sinus detect the dangerous hypertension and signal the brainstem.
  • The brainstem attempts compensatory measures via the vagus nerve (parasympathetic) *above* the level of injury: bradycardia, vasodilation (causing flushing, sweating, headache).
  • However, descending inhibitory signals from the brain cannot pass the SCI lesion to counteract the sympathetic surge *below* the level of injury.
  • Result: Continued hypertension below injury, bradycardia and vasodilation above injury. It's a medical emergency.
Clinical Manifestations:
  • Sudden, severe, pounding headache.
  • Marked, rapid increase in blood pressure (Systolic often >20-40 mmHg above baseline; can exceed 200-300 mmHg).
  • Bradycardia (most common, due to vagal response), but sometimes tachycardia.
  • Profuse sweating and flushing/blotching of the skin *above* the level of injury.
  • Pale, cool skin with piloerection (goosebumps) *below* the level of injury.
  • Nasal congestion.
  • Blurred vision or spots in the visual field.
  • Anxiety, apprehension, feeling of doom.
Critical Nursing Interventions:
  • Recognize AD immediately - it's an emergency!
  • 1. Elevate the Head of Bed (HOB) to 90 degrees (High Fowler's position) and lower the legs. (Uses orthostatic hypotension to help lower BP).
  • 2. Loosen any tight clothing, binders, or constrictive devices.
  • 3. Assess for and *remove* the triggering stimulus ASAP:
    • Bladder (Most common cause): Check for kinked catheter tubing, empty leg bag, assess for bladder distention. If catheterized, irrigate gently with small amount of sterile saline; if not patent, recatheterize immediately (use lidocaine jelly). If not catheterized, check for distention and catheterize.
    • Bowel (Second most common): Check for fecal impaction. Use anesthetic lubricant before digital check/removal, *but only after BP starts to lower* or if BP is stabilized with medication (digital stimulation can initially worsen AD).
    • Skin: Check for pressure areas, ingrown toenails, burns, tight shoes/clothing, skin breakdown.
  • 4. Monitor BP and HR frequently (every 2-5 minutes) until stable.
  • 5. Administer prescribed rapid-acting antihypertensive medications (e.g., nifedipine, nitrates, hydralazine) if symptoms persist despite removing stimulus or if stimulus cannot be immediately identified/removed. Follow institutional protocol.
  • 6. Document the event, triggers, interventions, and patient response.
  • 7. Educate the patient and caregivers about causes, symptoms, prevention (regular bowel/bladder care, skin checks), and emergency management of AD.

Question 5

Discuss the long-term rehabilitation goals and essential components of nursing interventions for a patient with a spinal cord injury at the level of paraplegia, addressing physical, psychosocial, and educational needs for successful community reintegration.

Potential Answer Points
Overall Long-Term Rehabilitation Goals (Paraplegia):
  • Maximize functional independence in mobility and self-care.
  • Prevent secondary complications (e.g., pressure injuries, UTIs, contractures, DVT/PE, chronic pain, depression).
  • Achieve effective bowel and bladder management.
  • Promote positive psychosocial adjustment and coping.
  • Facilitate successful reintegration into the home, community, work/school environments.
  • Optimize overall quality of life.
Essential Components & Nursing Interventions:
  • Physical Needs:
    • Mobility: Bed mobility, transfer training (bed, chair, car, toilet, tub), advanced wheelchair skills, potential for ambulation with bracing (depends on level/completeness). Interventions: Physical therapy collaboration, strengthening exercises (upper body crucial), balance training, practice, adaptive equipment assessment/training.
    • Self-Care (ADLs): Dressing, bathing, grooming, feeding. Interventions: Occupational therapy collaboration, teaching adaptive techniques, use of assistive devices.
    • Bowel Management: Establish consistent bowel program (timing, diet, stool softeners, digital stimulation/suppositories). Interventions: Education, assess effectiveness, troubleshoot problems (constipation/diarrhea), ensure privacy/routine.
    • Bladder Management: Typically involves intermittent catheterization (CIC) schedule or potentially indwelling/suprapubic catheter care. Interventions: Teach sterile/clean CIC technique, fluid management education, monitor for UTI signs/symptoms, assess residual volumes.
    • Skin Integrity: Prevent pressure injuries. Interventions: Teach regular pressure reliefs (weight shifts), skin inspection techniques (using mirrors), proper cushion/mattress use, nutrition/hydration education.
    • Preventing Complications: ROM exercises (prevent contractures), monitoring for DVT/PE (leg swelling, redness; prevention with medication/compression), managing spasticity (meds, stretching), managing neuropathic pain.
  • Psychosocial Needs:
    • Adjustment & Coping: Dealing with grief, loss, changes in body image, role changes. Interventions: Active listening, therapeutic communication, encourage expression of feelings, connect with peer support/counseling, involve psychology/social work, promote realistic hope.
    • Sexuality: Address concerns about sexual function and intimacy. Interventions: Provide information/resources, refer to specialists, create open/non-judgmental environment.
    • Social Interaction/Reintegration: Combat isolation, encourage participation in activities. Interventions: Explore accessible community resources, adaptive sports, support groups, assist with problem-solving barriers (transportation, accessibility).
    • Family Support: Educate family, address caregiver needs/stress. Interventions: Include family in teaching, provide resources, facilitate communication.
  • Educational Needs:
    • Condition Understanding: Anatomy/physiology of SCI, level of injury implications.
    • Self-Management Skills: Detailed teaching on bowel, bladder, skin care routines.
    • Complication Recognition/Management: Signs/symptoms of UTI, pressure injury, AD, DVT, and appropriate actions.
    • Medication Management: Purpose, dosage, side effects.
    • Health Promotion: Nutrition, hydration, exercise, importance of routine check-ups.
    • Resources: Information on equipment vendors, support organizations, financial aid, vocational rehab, legal rights (ADA).
    • Self-Advocacy: Empowering patient to manage their care and navigate healthcare/community systems.
Community Reintegration Focus: Interventions involve discharge planning, home accessibility assessment, coordination with community services, vocational counseling, driving assessment/adaptation exploration, ensuring follow-up care.
